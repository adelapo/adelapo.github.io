My Favorite Proofs

Proof 1: Prove that for all integers $n>2$, $\sqrt[n]{2}$ is irrational.

Use a proof by contradiction. Assume there exist integers $a,b$ such that $$\frac{a}{b}=\sqrt[n]{2}$$

The first step is to put each side to the power of $n$.

$$\left( \frac{a}{b} \right)^n = 2$$

That simplifies to

$$\frac{a^n}{b^n} = 2$$

Finally, multiply each side by $b^n$ to get

$$a^n = 2b^n$$

We know that $2b^n=b^n+b^n$. So the equation becomes

$$a^n = b^n + b^n$$

Recall Fermat's Last Theorem, which took 358 years to solve: there exists no such integer $n>2$ and integers $a,b,c$ such that $a^n+b^n=c^n$.

Applying Fermat's Last Theorem shows there are no integer solutions to $a^n = b^n + b^n$. This means there do not exist integers $a,b$ such that $\frac{a}{b}=\sqrt[n]{2}$, and so $\sqrt[n]{2}$ is irrational. $\square$
